Calculus result

Let a domain $\Omega\in \mathbb R^d$ be is an open, connected set.

The outward pointing unit normal vector field $n$ is defined along $\partial \Omega$. The unit normal at any point $x_0 \in \Omega$ is $n\left(x_0\right)$. If $u \in C^1(\bar{\Omega})$ then the outward normal derivative of $u$ is $\frac{\partial u}{\partial n}$, which is by definition $n \cdot \nabla u$. Let $d S(x)$ for the volume element of $\partial \Omega$, or just write $d S$.

The Divergence Theorem: let $\Omega$ be a domain with $\partial \Omega\in C^1$. Let $u \in C^1(\bar{\Omega})$. Then for $i=1, \ldots, d$, \begin{equation} \int_{\Omega} D_i u\; d x=\int_{\partial \Omega} u n_i\; d S(x) . \end{equation} The formula (1) is obtained from the Divergence Theorem by setting $$ \mathbf{u}=(0, \ldots, 0, u, 0, \ldots, 0) $$ and then the Divergence Theorem is \begin{equation} \int_{\Omega} \operatorname{div}(\mathbf{u}) d x=\int_{\partial \Omega} \mathbf{u} \cdot n\; d S(x), $\end{equation}

Integration by parts: suppose $u, v \in C^1(\bar{\Omega})$. Then for $i=1, \ldots, d$, \begin{equation} \int_{\Omega} D_i u v\; d x = -\int_{\Omega} u D_i v\; d x + \int_{\partial \Omega} u v n_i\; d S(x) . \end{equation} This follows immediately from (1) by applying $u v$ to it, noting $D_i(u v) = u D_i v + v D_i u$.

The Green's forumlas: suppose $u, v \in C^2(\Omega)$. Then we have \begin{eqnarray} \int_{\Omega} \Delta u\; d x &=& \int_{\partial \Omega} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}\; d S(x), \\ \int_{\Omega} \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\; d x &=& -\int_{\Omega} v \Delta u\; d x+\int_{\partial \Omega} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n} v\; d S(x), \\ \int_{\Omega} u \Delta v - v \Delta u\; d x &=& \int_{\partial \Omega} u \frac{\partial v}{\partial n} - v \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}\; d S(x) . \end{eqnarray}

Riesz Representation Theorem

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space over $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, and $T$ a bounded linear functional on $H$ (a bounded operator from $H$ to the field, $\mathbb K$, i.e., $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, over which $H$ is defined). The Riesz Representation Theorem is stated as followins:

Theorem. (The Riesz representation theorem)

If $T$ is a bounded linear functional on a Hilbert space $H$, $T: H \longrightarrow \mathbb K$, then there exists a unique $u \in H$ such that for every $v\in H$ we have $$ T(v)=\langle u , v\rangle $$ Moreover, $\|T\|=\|u\|$ (here $\|T\|$ denotes the operator norm of $T$, while $\|u\|$ is the Hilbert space norm of $u$.

Proof. Assume that the linear functional \(T\) is continuous. For the edge case where \(Tv=0\) for all \(v\), this is trivial because \(Tv=\left\langle v,0\right\rangle \), so \(v=0\). If not, we know that the null space of \(T\) i.e., \(N=\{v\mid Tv=0\}=T^{-1}\left(\{0\}\right)\) is closed because a continuous operator maps a closed set to a closed set i.e., \(\{0\}\). Since \(N\) is closed, the orthogonal complement \(N^{\perp}\) (subspace of elements orthogonal to \(N\)) contains elements other than \(0\) element. Consider \(h\in N^{\perp}\) such that \(\|h\|_{H}=1\). Consider \(f_{v}=\left(Tv\right)h-\left(Th\right)v\in N\) (i.e., \(Tf_{v}=0\)). Since \(h\) and \(f_{v}\) are orthogonal by construction, we have \[\begin{eqnarray*} 0 & = & \left\langle f_{v},h\right\rangle _{H}\\ & = & \left\langle \left(Tv\right)h-\left(Th\right)v,h\right\rangle _{H}\\ & = & \left(Tv\right)\|h\|^{2}-\left(Th\right)\left\langle v,h\right\rangle \\ & = & Tv-\left\langle v,(Th)h\right\rangle _{H}\\ \Rightarrow Tv & = & \left\langle v,(Th)h\right\rangle _{H}. \end{eqnarray*} \] Hence, \(u=\left(Th\right)h\) for some \(h\in N^{\perp}\). The element \(u\) is unique. If there were \(u_{1}\) and \(u_{2}\), then \(0=|Tv-Tv|=|\left\langle v,u_{1}-u_{2}\right\rangle |\leq\|v\|\|u_{1}-u_{2}\|\) by Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. This implies \(\|u_{1}-u_{2}\|=0\). Hence \(u_{1}=u_{2}\).

Sobolev space

A function $u \in L_{\mathrm{loc}}^1(\Omega)$ is called $k$ times weakly differentiable if all its weak derivatives exist for $|\alpha| \leq k$. Let $W^k(\Omega)$ denote the set of $k$ times weakly differentiable functions: $$ W^k(\Omega): = \left\{u \in L_{\text {loc }}^1(\Omega) \mid u \text { is } k \text { times weakly differentiable }\right\} $$ Note: $C^k(\Omega) \subseteq W^k(\Omega)$.

(Sobolev space)

Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$. Let $1 \leq p\lt \infty$ and $k \in \mathbb{N}$. Define the Sobolev space $$ W^{k, p}(\Omega):=\left\{u \in W^k(\Omega) \mid D^\alpha u \in L^p(\Omega) \text { for all }|\alpha| \leq k\right\} $$ (Note: if $\alpha=(0, \ldots, 0)$ and $u \in W^{k, p}(\Omega) \Rightarrow u \in L^p(\Omega)$ ).

We define a norm on $W^{k, p}(\Omega)$ by $$ \|u\|_{W^{k, p}(\Omega)}:=\left(\sum_{|\alpha| \leq k} \int_{\Omega}\left|D^\alpha u\right|^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} . $$

Remark: For triangle inequality, we take $u, v \in W^{k, p}(\Omega)$. Then by Minkowski's inequality, $$ \begin{aligned} \|u+v\|_{W^{k, p}(\Omega)} &=\left(\sum_{|\alpha| \leq k}\left\|D^\alpha u+D^\alpha v\right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} \\ & \leq\left(\sum_{|\alpha| \leq k}\left(\left\|D^\alpha u\right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}+\left\|D^\alpha v\right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}\right)^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} \\ & \leq\left(\sum_{|\alpha| \leq k}\left\|D^\alpha u\right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}+\left(\sum_{|\alpha| \leq k}\left\|D^\alpha v\right\|_{L^p(\Omega)}^p\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} \\ &=\|u\|_{W^{k, p}(\Omega)}+\|v\|_{W^{k, p}(\Omega)} \cdot \end{aligned} $$
