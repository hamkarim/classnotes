ការវែកញែកនៅក្រោមភាពមិនច្បាស់លាស់ អាចឱ្យគេរុករក និងធ្វើឱ្យយល់ អំពីពិភពមួយ ដែលជារឿយៗមិនអាចទាយទុកជាមុនបាន និងមិនពេញលេញ។
  • In many settings, we must try to understand what is going on in a system when we have imperfect or incomplete information.
  • Two reasons why we might reason under uncertainty:
    • laziness (modeling every detail of a complex system is costly)
    • ignorance (we may not completely understand the system)

Using Probability Theory to reason under uncertainty

  • Probabilities quantify uncertainty regarding the occurrence of events.
  • Are there alternatives? Yes, e.g., Dempster-Shafer Theory, disjunctive uncertainty, etc. (Fuzzy Logic is about imprecision, not uncertainty.)
  • Why is Probability Theory better? Because if you do not reason according to Probability Theory, you can be made to act irrationally (de Finetti)
  • Probability Theory is key to the study of action and communication:
    • Decision Theory: combines Probability Theory with Utility Theory.
    • Information Theory: is "the logarithm of Probability Theory".
  • Probability Theory gives rise to many interesting and important philosophical questions (which we will not cover).

១.១ ប្រូបាប៊ីលីតេបឋមៗ (elementary probability)

គោលគំនិតសំខាន់ៗជាច្រើននៅក្នុងប្រូបាប៊ីលីតេ អាចឱ្យគេយល់បានតាមរយៈសេណារីយ៉ូសាមញ្ញៗ ជាមូលហេតុមួយ ដែលគេចាប់ផ្តើមដោយសិក្សាទ្រឹស្តីប្រូបាប៊ីលីតេបឋមៗ គឺពិតជាមានប្រយោជន៍ណាស់។ ចំណោទប្រូបាប៊ីលីតេ ពិតជាមានល្បិចច្រើន ហើយការដែលមានវិធីសាស្រ្តច្បាស់លាស់ ក្នុងការដោះស្រាយចំណោទ គឺពិតជាចាំបាច់។ ខាងក្រោម ជាឧទាហរណ៍ងាយមួយ ដើម្បីបញ្ជាក់រឿងនេះ ៖

ចំណោទ៖ គេដកសន្លឹកបៀទាំងអស់ចេញពីហ៊ូបៀមួយ លើកលែងតែសន្លឹកអាត់ (A) និងសន្លឹកស្តេច (K) ។ ដូច្នេះគេនៅសល់តែប្រាំបីសន្លឹកប៉ុណ្ណោះ អាត់បួនសន្លឹក (4A) និងស្តេចបួនសន្លឹក (4K) ។ នៅក្នុងហ៊ូបៀតូចនេះ អ្នកចែកបៀពីរសន្លឹកទៅឱ្យមិត្តភ័ក្តិ ។

If your friend looks at his cards and announces (truthfully) that his hand contains an ace, what is the probability that both his cards are aces? If he announces instead that one of his cards is the ace of spades, what is the probability then that both his cards are aces? Are these two probabilities the same?

Probability theory helps us structure our approach to problems like these by distinguishing between two essential components:

  1. Events: Collections of possible outcomes.
  2. Probabilities: Numbers assigned to these events.

Example Analysis in Detail

ករណីទី១: Announcement of an Ace: If your friend announces he has an ace, what is the probability that both his cards are aces?
  1. Total Possible Hands: To determine the total number of possible hands (combinations of two cards from eight), we use the combination formula: $$\binom{8}{2} = \frac{8!}{2!(8-2)!} = \frac{8 \times 7}{2 \times 1} = 28$$ So, there are $28$ possible hands.
  2. Hands with No Aces: To find the number of hands with no aces, we look at the combinations of two cards chosen from the four kings: $$\binom{4}{2} = \frac{4!}{2!(4-2)!} = \frac{4 \times 3}{2 \times 1} = 6$$ So, there are 6 hands with no aces.
  3. Hands with At Least One Ace: Subtract the number of hands with no aces from the total number of hands: $$28 - 6 = 22$$ So, there are $22$ hands with at least one ace. Since your friend has an ace, his hand is one of these $22$.
  4. Hands with Two Aces: To find the number of hands with two aces, we look at the combinations of two cards chosen from the four aces: $$ \binom{4}{2} = 6 $$ Therefore, there are 6 hands containing two aces.
    5. Now, the probability that your friend has two aces, given that he has at least one ace, is: $$\frac{6}{22} = \frac{3}{11}$$

ករណីទី២: Announcement of Ace of Spades: If your friend announces that one of his cards is the ace of spades, what is the probability that both his cards are aces?

  1. Total Possibilities for the Second Card: If one card is the ace of spades, there are 7 remaining cards from which to choose the second card.
  2. Hands with Two Aces: Out of the 7 remaining cards, the other aces are the ace of hearts, ace of diamonds, and ace of clubs. So there are 3 cards that will result in having two aces. Therefore, the probability is: $\frac{3}{7}$.

ការសន្មត់ (implicit Assumptions):

In both scenarios, an implicit assumption is that all hands are equally likely. This means each combination of two cards has an equal probability of being dealt.
By breaking down the problem into smaller parts and using probability theory, we can systematically organize our thinking and calculations. This helps us better understand the complexities involved, even in seemingly simple scenarios.

(a) លំហសំណាក (sample space)

The basic setting for a probability model is the random experiment or random trial. This serves as your mental model of what is happening in the scenario. For example, in the previous card example, the random experiment is the dealer passing two cards to your friend.

  1. ពិសោធន៍ចៃដន្យ (random experiment): It is a procedure or process that leads to one or more outcomes that cannot be predicted with certainty.
    Each time the experiment is conducted, it may produce different results, even under the same conditions. Examples include rolling a die, flipping a coin, or drawing a card from a deck. The key characteristic of a random experiment is the inherent variability in the outcomes.
  2. ពិសោធន៍ (experiment): In probability theory, an experiment refers to any process or activity that generates data.
    This term is often used interchangeably with "random experiment," but it can also refer to non-randomized studies or controlled experiments in statistics. In probability, however, an experiment typically focuses on the variability and unpredictability of outcomes.
  3. Random Trial: is a specific type of random experiment that is characterized by the following features:

    Essentially, a random trial is a single instance of performing a random experiment, and it is used to analyze the probability of different outcomes occurring. For example, tossing a coin once is a random trial, while tossing it multiple times constitutes a series of random trials.
    • Repetition: A random trial can be repeated multiple times under the same conditions.
    • Randomness: Each trial produces outcomes that are determined by chance.
    • Well-defined outcomes: The possible outcomes of the trial are known and can be listed, such as the results of flipping a coin (heads or tails) or rolling a die (numbers 1 through 6).
(Sample Space - $S$ or $\Omega$)
The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Depending on the nature of the random experiment, the sample space \(S\) can be:
  1. រាប់អស់ (Finite): A limited number of outcomes.
  2. រាប់មិនអស់តែរាប់បាន (Countably Infinite): An infinite set of outcomes that can be listed.
  3. រាប់មិនបាន (Uncountably Infinite): An infinite set of outcomes that cannot be listed.
Here are some examples to illustrate these concepts:
  1. Random Coin Toss: The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes, which are heads (H) and tails (T). Thus: \[ S = \{H, T\} \] The size of the sample space is \(|S| = 2\).
  2. Card Example: In the card example, where only aces and kings remain in the deck, the sample space consists of all possible unordered pairs of cards. If you only consider the combinations without regard to order: \[ |S| = 28 \] This represents choosing 2 cards from 8 without considering the order. However, if you decide to include the order in which the two cards are dealt, the sample space doubles. For instance, it would include both (Ace of Hearts, King of Spades) and (King of Spades, Ace of Hearts). So, the size of the sample space would be: \[ |S| = 56 \]

This shows that there is some degree of artistry in probability theory, especially in how to choose the most appropriate sample space.

More Examples.
  1. ក្រឡុកគ្រាប់ឡុកឡាក់មួយ (Roll a die):
    • Experiment: Rolling a single six-sided die.
    • Sample Space: The outcome is the number on the upturned face. \[ S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\} \] The size of the sample space is \(|S| = 6\).
  2. បោះកាក់រហូតចេញខាងរូប (Toss a coin until Heads appears):
    • Experiment: Tossing a coin until the first head appears.
    • Sample Space: The outcome is the number of tosses required. \[ S = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\} \] The size of the sample space is countably infinite (\(|S| = \infty\)).
  3. ជ្រើសរើសលេខចៃដន្យនៅចន្លោះ $0$ និង $1$ (Choose a random number between 0 and 1):
    • Experiment: Selecting a random number from the interval [0, 1].
    • Sample Space: \[ S = [0, 1] \] This is an example of an uncountable sample space.
  4. គប់​ព្រួញ​មួយ​ទៅលើ​ក្ដារ​រាងមូល (Throw a dart at a circular dartboard):
    • Experiment: Throwing a dart at a circular dartboard of radius 1.
    • Sample Space: The coordinates (x, y) where the dart lands within the circle. \[ S = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid x^2 + y^2 \leq 1\} \]

For this review of elementary probability, we will focus on finite and countably infinite sample spaces. This simplification makes it easier to understand and calculate probabilities.

By clearly defining the sample space, we lay the groundwork for more advanced probability calculations. It helps us to precisely understand and structure the possible outcomes of any random experiment.

(b) Events

An event is a collection of possible outcomes from a random experiment. These outcomes are usually denoted by letters such as \(A\), \(B\), etc.

Formally, an event \(A\) is a subset of the sample space \(S\), so \(A \subseteq S\). An event typically includes the set of outcomes that make the answer to a particular question 'Yes'. Saying 'the outcome is in \(A\)' is equivalent to saying 'the event \(A\) is true'.

For instance, in our card example, one event of interest is having both cards as aces. This event includes all outcomes that fulfill this condition, specifically the 6 hands that consist of two aces. There are two special events:

  • The certain event or sure event: This is the entire sample space \(S\).
  • The null event: This is the empty set \(\emptyset\), which contains no outcomes.

សង្ខេប (Sample Spaces and Events)

  • An experiment is a process whose outcome can be observed.
  • The sample space, $S$ or $\Omega$, of an experiment is the set of possible outcomes for the experiment. (The sample space may be finite or infinite).
  • An EVENT is a subset of the sample space, i.e. a (possibly empty) collection of possible outcomes to the experiment. We say that an event A OCCURS if the outcome is an element of $A$.

Note: The difference between an outcome and an event in this context is that an outcome is an individual possible result of the experiment; an event may include many different possible outcomes.

Experiment: Toss a coin three times.
  • The Sample Space is $$S=\Bigl\{{HHH}, {HHT}, {HTH}, {HTT}, {THH}, {THT}, {TTH}, {TTT}\Bigr\}$$
  • The event "2 heads, one tail" is the subset $$A=\{H H T, H T H, T H H\} \; \text{ of } S$$
