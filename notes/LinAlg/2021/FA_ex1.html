Functional Anaysis: TD & TP (I)

October 2nd, 2020

  Function spaces

Continuous maps

A map $f: X \rightarrow Y$ from one metric space to another is continuous at $x \in X$ if, for every $\varepsilon\gt 0$, there is $\delta\gt 0$ such that $\d_{X}\left(x, x^{\prime}\right)\lt \delta$ implies $\d_{Y}\left(f(x), f\left(x^{\prime}\right)\right)\lt \varepsilon$.

The map $f: X \rightarrow Y$ is continuous if it is continuous at every point of $X$.

Proposition: A map $f: X \rightarrow Y$ continuous if and only if, for every Cauchy sequence $\left\{x_{i}\right\}$ in $X$ converging to $x \in X$, $$ \lim _{i} f\left(x_{i}\right)=f\left(\lim _{i} x_{i}\right) $$
Show solution

Function spaces $C^{0}[a, b]$: Read now!

Denote $C^{0}[a, b]$ as the space of continuous real-valued functions on a finite interval $[a, b] \subset \mathbb{R}$. It has a natural metric $$ \d(f, g)=\sup _{x \in[a, b]}|f(x)-g(x)| \quad \text { (sup-norm metric) } $$ Surely, $C^{0}[a, b]$ is complete in the sense that any Cauchy sequence converges to another function in this space.

This is the idea! For a sequence $\left\{f_{i}\right\}$ such that, for every $\varepsilon\gt 0$, there is $i_{o}$ such that $\d\left(f_{i}, f_{j}\right)\lt \varepsilon$ for all $i, j \geq i_{o}$, then $\lim _{i} f_{i}$ is an element of $C^{0}[a, b]$. Where the limit is a function evaluated pointwise by $$ \left(\lim _{i} f_{i}\right)(x)=\lim _{i} f_{i}(x) \quad \text { (for } x \in[a, b]) $$

Indeed, the pointwise limits $f(x)=\lim _{i} f_{i}(x)$ exist, since $$\left|f_{i}(x)-f_{j}(x)\right| \leq \sup _{y}\left|f_{i}(y)-f_{j}(y)\right|$$ shows that the pointwise values are Cauchy sequences of real numbers. This is to see that the pointwise-limit function $f$ is continuous.

Continuity is a three-epsilon argument, once uniform continuity on $[a, b]$ is observed.

Given $\varepsilon\gt 0$, take $i_{o}$ such that $\d\left(f_{i}, f_{j}\right)\lt \varepsilon$ for all $i, j \geq i_{o}$. Just take $i=i_{o}$. Since $f_{i}$ is uniformly continuous on $[a, b]$, take $\delta\gt 0$ such that $|x-y|\lt \delta$ implies $\left|f_{i}(x)-f_{i}(y)\right|\lt \varepsilon$. The triangle inequality for the usual absolute value on real numbers gives \begin{eqnarray*} |f(x)-f(y)| & \leq& \left|f(x)-f_{i}(x)\right|+\left|f_{i}(x)-f_{i}(y)\right|+ \left|f_{i}(y)-f(y)\right| \\ & \lt & \varepsilon+\varepsilon+\varepsilon \end{eqnarray*} This proves that the pointwise limit function $f$ is continuous.

Like the standard metric on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$, another reasonable metric on $C^{0}[a, b]$ is $$ \d_{2}(f, g)=\left(\int_{a}^{b}|f(x)-g(x)|^{2} d x\right)^{\!\frac{1}{2}} \qquad\left(L^{2} \text {-norm metric }\right) $$

Problem 1 : (not a complete space)

With the $L^{2}$-norm metric above, show that $C^{0}[a, b]$ is not complete.

HINT: Take a piecewise linear continuous functions by $$ f_{n}(x)=\left\{ \begin{array}{lcl} 0 & , & \left(\text { for } a \leq x \leq \frac{a+b}{2}-\frac{1}{n}\right) \\ \frac{n}{2} \cdot\left[x-\left(\frac{a+b}{2}-\frac{1}{n}\right)\right] & , & \left(\text { for } \frac{a+b}{2}-\frac{1}{n} \leq x \leq \frac{a+b}{2}+\frac{1}{n}\right) \\ 1 & , & \left(\text { for } \frac{a+b}{2}+\frac{1}{n} \leq x \leq b\right) \end{array}\right. $$ Then show the discountinuity! Draw it if possible!

Sequence spaces $\ell^{p}$

An infinite-dimensional which is analogue of the standard metric on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ is the set $$ \ell^{2}=\Big\{x=\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots\right): \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\left|x_{i}\right|^{2} \lt \infty\Big\} $$ with metric $$ \d_{2}(x, y)=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\left|x_{i}-y_{i}\right|^{2}} $$

If replacing $2$ by any $1 \leq p\lt\infty$, it works as well: $$ \ell^{p}=\Big\{x=\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots\right): \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\left|x_{i}\right|^{p}\lt \infty\Big\} $$ with metric $$ \d_{p}(x, y)=\left(\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\left|x_{i}-y_{i}\right|^{p}\right)^{1 / p} $$ Slightly unlike the case of varying metrics on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$, the underlying sets $\ell^{p}$ are not the same. For example, $\ell^{2}$ is strictly larger than $\ell^{1}$.

Problem 3 : (Compactness)

Let $X=\ell^2$.   Inner Product Spaces and Norm Spaces

Let \(X\) be a vector space over \(\K \in \{\R,\C\}\).

(Inner-product spaces)

An inner product \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\) on \(X\) is a map \[\begin{array}{ccl} \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: & X \times X & \longrightarrow & \mathbb K\\ & (x,y) & \longmapsto & \langle x,y \rangle \end{array} \] defined with the axioms as follows: for \(x,y,z \in X\) and \(\lambda \in \K\) arbitrary,

  1. Conjugate symmetric: \[ \langle x,y \rangle = \overline{\langle y,x \rangle}\]
  2. Linear in its first argument: \[ \begin{align*} \langle \lambda x,y \rangle &= \lambda \langle x,y \rangle,\\ \langle x + y, z \rangle &= \langle x , z \rangle + \langle y, z \rangle, \end{align*} \]
  3. and non-degenerate (positive definite): \[ \langle x, x \rangle \gt 0 \quad \text{ for }\quad x \neq 0, \]
The pair \(\big(X,\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\big)\) is called an inner-product space.

Any inner product space is a normed linear space. But, a normed linear space is not necessarily an inner product space. For example, for $x\in \mathbb C^n$, \[ \| x \|_{\infty} = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |x_i| \] The max norm cannot be given by an inner product.

Norms Induced by Inner Products: Parallelogram Law

Let $(X,\Vert \cdot \Vert)$ be linear space. Then, the norm $\|\cdot\|$ satisfies the parallelogram law $$2 \Vert x \Vert^2 + 2\Vert y \Vert^2 = \Vert x + y \Vert^2 + \Vert x - y \Vert^2$$ for all $x,y \in X$ and the inducing inner production is $$\langle x, y \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \left( \Vert x + y \Vert^2 - \Vert x - y \Vert^2\right).$$

Problem 4 : (no inner product on a set)

Given the linear space $X=\mathcal C[0,1]$ equipped with the norm $$ \|f\|_{1}=\int_{0}^{1}|f(x)| \d x $$ With such norm, show that there is no inner product on $X$ ?

HINT: It is required to show that $\|\cdot\|_1$ does not satisfy the parallelogram law, with known countinuous functions on $[0,1]$.

Show solution
