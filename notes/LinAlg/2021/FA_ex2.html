Operator Norm

Differentiation in norm spaces

REM.: A mapping $f : X \longrightarrow Y$ can be continuous in one topological space, yet fails in another one.

Let see both norms $\|\cdot \|_0$ and $\|\cdot\|_1$ defined in below exerice for this kind of remark.

Problem 1 : (Different spaces, different continuities)

Let $X=\mathcal C^{\infty}\big([0,2 \pi], \mathbb{R}\big)$ be the vector space of differentiable functions $f:[0,2 \pi] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ of class $\mathcal C^{\infty}$. For $f \in X$, let define $$ \begin{aligned} \|f\|_{0} &=\sup \Big\{|f(x)| \mid x \in[0,2 \pi]\Big\} \\ \|f\|_{1} &=\sup \Big\{|f(x)|+\left|f^{\prime}(x)\right| \mid x \in[0,2 \pi]\Big\} \end{aligned} $$ Next, we define the linear mapping $\mathcal D: X \rightarrow X$ as follows, $$ \mathcal D(f)=f^{\prime} \quad \text { \; for\; } f \in X $$

  1. Show that $\|\cdot\|_{0}$ and $\|\cdot\|_{1}$ are norms in $X$.
  2. Show that for every $n \in \mathbb{N}$ there exists a function $f_{n} \in X$ for which $$\left\|f_{n}\right\|_{0}=0\quad \text{ and } \quad \left\|D\left(f_{n}\right)\right\|_{0}=n$$ In such case, show that $\mathcal D: X \rightarrow X$ is not continuous, when in space $\big(X, \|\cdot\|_{0}\big)$.
  3. Show that $\mathcal D: X_{1} \rightarrow X_{0}$ is continuous, where we denote $X_{1}$ as $\big(X, \|\cdot\|_{1}\big)$ and $X_{0}$ as $\big(X, ,\|\cdot\|_{0}\big)$.

HINT: For quetion (ii), a trigonometric function is preferred $$ f_{n}(x)=\sin n x, \quad x \in[0,2 \pi] $$ Then, let the sequence $g_{n}=\frac{1}{n} f_{n}$ as in the need.

Show solution

No way!

For a continuous function $f:[0,1] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, we set $$ \|f\|_{1}=\int_{0}^{1}|f(x)| \d x $$ We consider $X$ as a normed vector space with the norm $\|\cdot\|_{1}$ and define the function $$ \mathcal I: C([0,1], \mathbb{R}) \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \quad \text { by } \quad \mathcal I(f)=\int_{0}^{1} f(x) \d x $$

Problem 4 : (Compactness of integral operator)

Suppose $X,Y= L^2(0,1)$ and let a function $K \in \mathcal C^0([0,1]^2)$. We define an operator $$A: X\to Y \quad \text{ by }\quad Af(x):=\int_0^1 K(x,y)f(y)\d y$$ $\forall\, f\in \mathcal L^2(0,1)$. Show that $A$ is compact.

HINT: To show that $A$ is compact, you can use elementary arguments or

Show solution

No way!

To show that $A$ is compact using elementary arguments, let $\{ f_{n} \}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ be a bounded sequence in $L^{2}[0,1]$ with $\|f_{n}\| \le M$. For every $\epsilon \gt 0$, there exists $\delta \gt 0$ such that $|K(x,y)-K(x',y')| \lt \epsilon$ whenever $|x-x'|+|y-y'| \lt \delta$. Therefore, $\big\{ Af_{n}\big\}$ is a sequence of continuous functions for which $$\begin{aligned} |Af_{n}(x)-Af_{n}(x')| & \le \int_{0}^{1}|K(x,y)-K(x',y)||f_{n}(y)|\,\d y \\ & \le \epsilon \int_{0}^{1}|f_{n}(y)|\,\d y \\ & \le \epsilon\|1\|\|f_{n}\| \le M\epsilon,\;\;\; |x-x'| \lt \delta \end{aligned}$$ That shows that $\big\{ Af_{n} \big\}$ is an equicontinuous family of continuous functions on $[0,1]$. So, there exists a subsequence $\{ Af_{n_{k}}\}$ that converges uniformly to a continuous function $g$. Since uniform convergence implies convergence in $L^{2}[0,1]$, it follows that $\{ A f_{n_{k}}\}$ converges in $L^{2}[0,1]$. Therefore $A$ is compact because the image of a bounded sequence always contains a convergent subsequence.

Actually, if $k\in L^2(0,1)^2$, then you may consider $K$ as a limit of a sequence of finite rank operators. Since $L^2(0,1)$ is a separable Hilbert space, there exists a Hilbert basis denoted by $\{e_i\}_{i=1}^{\infty}$. Define $T_n (n\in\mathbb{N}^{+})$ as $$ T_nf:=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\langle f, e_i \rangle e_i,\quad \forall f\in L^{2}(0,1). $$ Then, $K_n:=T_nKT_n$ is a finite rank operator. And $K_n$ is compact for each $n$. You need to show that $\lim\limits_{n\rightarrow \infty}||K_n-K||=0$. Then, you conclude that $K$ is compact, since the space of compact operators mapping $L^2(0,1)$ into $L^2(0,1)$ is closed.
